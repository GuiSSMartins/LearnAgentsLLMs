In its familiar form, Newton's third law is not always valid. It is an excellent approximation in classical mechanics, but there are important situations where it must be modified or replaced.

Here's the breakdown:

When it is valid

Newton's third law states:

If object A exerts a force on object B, then object B exerts an equal and opposite force on object A.

This works very well for:

Everyday mechanics (cars, balls, people pushing on walls)
Rigid bodies
Most engineering problems
Situations where forces act essentially instantaneously

For example, if you push on a wall with 100 N, the wall pushes back on you with 100 N.

When it breaks down
1. Electromagnetic interactions

If charged particles are moving, the forces between them are carried by electromagnetic fields that propagate at the speed of light.

At a given instant:

The force on A due to B may not be exactly equal and opposite to the force on B due to A.
The electromagnetic field itself carries momentum.

The total momentum of particles + field is still conserved, even though the simple third-law pair may not hold.

2. Relativity

In Special Relativity, information cannot travel faster than light. Since interactions are not instantaneous, the traditional statement of Newton's third law is no longer fundamental.

Instead, conservation of momentum arises from deeper principles in relativistic physics.

3. Quantum physics

In quantum mechanics, particles interact through quantum fields rather than classical forces. Newton's laws are no longer the basic description, although they emerge approximately at macroscopic scales.

What always survives?

The more fundamental principle is conservation of momentum.

According to Noether's theorem:

If the laws of physics are the same everywhere in space (translational symmetry),
then total momentum is conserved.

Newton's third law is one way to guarantee momentum conservation in simple classical systems, but it is not the deepest physical law.

Summary
Situation	Third law valid?
Everyday mechanics	✅ Yes
Classical contact forces	✅ Yes
Gravity (Newtonian)	✅ Yes
Electromagnetic systems with radiation or time delays	❌ Not always
Relativistic physics	❌ Not fundamental
Quantum physics	❌ Replaced by quantum field theory

So the answer is: No. Newton's third law is not universally valid. It is an excellent classical approximation, while conservation of momentum is the more fundamental principle that remains valid in modern physics.
